BC Population Change by Component, 2019-2025

Annual contribution by component (stacked columns) with total population change overlay (black line).

Source: Major data file.xlsx

Executive Takeaway

From 2019 to 2025, the composition of population change shifted away from natural increase and toward migration. Natural increase is small and negative in multiple years, while international migration and net non-permanent resident (NPR) change account for most net growth in 2022-2023. Interprovincial migration contributes materially in 2020-2021 but is near zero or negative in 2023-2024. The 2025 net decline is driven primarily by a large negative NPR change, showing that NPR is the most volatile component in the period.

BC Population Trends: Total, Working-Age, and Seniors (2019=100)

Indexed trends (2019=100) to compare headline population growth with labour-market-relevant growth.

Source: Major data file.xlsx, LFS (2019-CSV to 2025-CSV)

Executive Takeaway

Indexed to 2019, seniors (65+) grow materially faster than the working-age population over 2019-2025. The working-age index rises steadily, but at a slower pace than seniors, so the age structure shifts older even during periods of overall growth. This pattern implies that a larger share of population change is occurring outside core labour-market ages, strengthening replacement demand and reducing how much total population growth translates into effective labour supply growth.

Working-age definition used: 15-64. Seniors: 65+. Values use available LFS microdata through 2025.

Executive Takeaway

Population scale remains concentrated in Mainland/Southwest, but growth rates differ by region and by year. In 2025, growth is comparatively more balanced across regions, and Mainland/Southwest posts one of the lowest growth rates (lower than every region except Nechako). Sub-Regional Population Growth Rates and Weighted BC Average

Thin smoothed lines: annual sub-region (regional district) growth rates. Thick smoothed line: weighted average across all sub-regions.

Source: pop_municipal_subprov_areas.xlsx (Dev-RD)

Executive Takeaway

Sub-regional population growth is highly uneven across years. Province-wide average growth can remain moderate while individual sub-regions experience sharp accelerations or slowdowns, indicating that growth is spatially concentrated rather than broadly distributed. Periods of wider dispersion are consistent with localized labour-market pressure, tighter housing and service capacity in fast-growth areas, and stronger matching frictions between available workers and vacancies across locations.

Labour Market Analytics

Labour Market Impacts

Focus on labour supply-demand tension: unemployment conditions versus demand pressure to surface whether BC is facing broad scarcity or matching friction.

BC Unemployed Persons and Vacant Jobs (Monthly)

Monthly levels: unemployed persons vs vacant jobs, 2019-01 to 2025-12.

Source: LFS (2019-CSV to 2025-CSV), Job Vacancies.csv

Note: the Vacancy-to-unemployment ratio line is hidden by default. Click its legend item to toggle it on.

Executive Takeaway

Monthly levels show tightening into 2022 followed by cooling through 2024-2025: vacancies peak and then trend down while unemployed persons trend up. The vacancy-to-unemployment ratio summarizes the shift, peaking in 2022-09 (above 1) and declining to about 0.37 by late 2025. Even in the cooling phase, vacancies remain non-trivial alongside elevated unemployment, a pattern consistent with matching frictions by occupation, skill, and geography rather than a single province-wide shortage or surplus.

Monthly series now extends through 2025 for both unemployed persons and vacant jobs, enabling a current view of labour demand-supply tension.

Labour Market Status by Key Population Group, 2025

2025 average monthly levels by group: employed persons and unemployed persons.

Source: LFS (2025-CSV)

Executive Takeaway

Employment and unemployment are unevenly distributed across groups in 2025. Core-age workers account for the majority of employment and also the largest absolute number of unemployed persons, reflecting their scale in the labour force. Youth have a smaller employed base with a sizable unemployed count, consistent with weaker attachment at labour-market entry. Recent immigrants contribute materially to both employment and unemployment, consistent with integration frictions that can persist even when employment volumes are high.

Group definitions: Recent immigrants = landed within 10 years (IMMIG=1); Youth = 15-24; Core-age = 25-54; Older workers = 55+.

Labour Force Ages 55+: Annual Growth Decomposition

Annual growth-rate view (2020-2025): employed 55+ and unemployed 55+ growth bars with labour force 55+ growth line.

Source: Labour Force Characteristics Total.csv

Executive Takeaway

The 55+ labour force and 55+ employment growth rates are relatively stable across most years, indicating gradual structural change. In contrast, unemployed 55+ growth is much more volatile, with large swings in downturn and recovery years. This divergence shows that the size of the older labour force can remain steady while labour market conditions for older job seekers change quickly. It is consistent with replacement-demand pressure from aging coexisting with cyclical re-employment dynamics for older workers.

Bars show annual growth rates of employed and unemployed ages 55+; the line shows annual growth of total labour force ages 55+.

Industry Average Worker Age, 2025

Horizontal ranking of industries by estimated average worker age (2025).

Source: Labour Force Characteristics Industries.csv

Executive Takeaway

Average worker age varies meaningfully by industry in 2025. Industries at the older end of the ranking face higher near-term retirement exposure and replacement demand risk, while younger-profile industries typically have more entry-level churn dynamics. The ranking helps identify where workforce renewal pressures are likely to be strongest, and where the dominant risk is succession versus retention.

Method: average age estimated by weighting 15-24, 25-54, and 55+ labour-force counts with age-group midpoints.

Industry Labour Force Gender Gap, 2025

Horizontal ranking from highest to lowest gender gap across industries.

Source: Labour Force Characteristics Industries.csv

Executive Takeaway

Gender concentration is large in a subset of industries and much smaller in others, indicating strong sector-level segmentation. In high-gap industries, the effective recruiting pool is structurally narrower, which can contribute to persistent vacancy pressure when demand rises. The ordering provides a clear map of where the gender distribution is most imbalanced and where targeted pipeline expansion would likely have the highest leverage.

Gap metric: absolute difference between men+ and women+ labour-force shares within each industry (percentage points).

Future Trends

Strategic forward lens on migration composition and volatility to help leadership track turning points in labour-market and service-demand conditions.

BC Net Migration by Source, 2019-2025

Net migration source split, annual 2019-2025.

Source: Major data file.xlsx

Executive Takeaway

Net migration composition shifts materially across 2019-2025. International migration and NPR change dominate the post-pandemic rebound, while interprovincial migration is smaller and more episodic. NPR change is the most volatile series and drives much of the year-to-year swing, including the 2025 reversal. This composition matters because volatility in the dominant component translates into volatility in net population growth.

Executive Takeaway
