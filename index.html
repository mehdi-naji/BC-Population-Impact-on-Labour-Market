Population Analytics

Shift in Population Drivers

BC Population Change by Component, 2019-2025

Display unit

Source: Government of British Columbia population statistics portal; Quarterly population and migration estimates for B.C.

Executive Takeaway

From 2019 to 2025, the composition of population change shifted away from natural increase and toward migration. Natural increase is small and negative in multiple years, while international migration and net non-permanent resident (NPR) change account for most net growth in 2022-2023. Interprovincial migration contributes materially in 2020-2021 but is near zero or negative in 2023-2024. The 2025 net decline is driven primarily by a large negative NPR change, showing that NPR is the most volatile component in the period.

BC Population Trends: Total, Working-Age, and Seniors (2019=100)

Source: Government of British Columbia population statistics portal; Quarterly population and migration estimates for B.C., Statistics Canada, Labour Force Survey (years 2019 - 2025)

Executive Takeaway

Indexed to 2019, seniors (65+) grow materially faster than the working-age population over 2019-2025. The working-age index rises steadily, but at a slower pace than seniors, so the age structure shifts older even during periods of overall growth. This pattern implies that a larger share of population change is occurring outside core labour-market ages, strengthening replacement demand and reducing how much total population growth translates into effective labour supply growth.

Working-age definition used: 15-64. Seniors: 65+. Values use available LFS microdata through 2025.

BC Regional Population Levels and Annual Growth

Year

Source: Government of British Columbia population statistics portal; Municipal and sub-provincial areas population, 2011 - 2025

Executive Takeaway

Population scale remains concentrated in Mainland/Southwest, but growth rates differ by region and by year. In 2025, growth is comparatively more balanced across regions, and Mainland/Southwest posts one of the lowest growth rates (lower than every region except Nechako). Most other regions show modest positive growth, which implies that incremental population change in 2025 is less concentrated in the largest region than in earlier high-growth years.

Geography note: Regional buckets are built from BC Stats Development Regions in your source file.

Sub-Regional Population Growth Rates and Weighted BC Average

Source: Government of British Columbia population statistics portal; Municipal and sub-provincial areas population, 2011 - 2025

Executive Takeaway

Sub-regional population growth is highly uneven across years. Province-wide average growth can remain moderate while individual sub-regions experience sharp accelerations or slowdowns, indicating that growth is spatially concentrated rather than broadly distributed. Periods of wider dispersion are consistent with localized labour-market pressure, tighter housing and service capacity in fast-growth areas, and stronger matching frictions between available workers and vacancies across locations.

Labour Market Analytics

Labour Market Impacts

BC Unemployed Persons and Vacant Jobs (Monthly)

Source: Statistics Canada, Labour Force Survey (years 2019 - 2025), Statistics Canada, Table 14-10-0371-01, Job vacancies, payroll employees, and job vacancy rate by provinces and territories

Note: the Unemployment-to-vacancy ratio line is hidden by default. Click its legend item to toggle it on.

Executive Takeaway

Monthly levels show tightening into 2022 followed by cooling through 2024-2025: vacancies peak and then trend down while unemployed persons trend up. The unemployment-to-vacancy ratio summarizes the shift, rising above 2 by late 2025. Even in the cooling phase, vacancies remain non-trivial alongside elevated unemployment, a pattern consistent with matching frictions by occupation, skill, and geography rather than a single province-wide shortage or surplus.

Monthly series now extends through 2025 for both unemployed persons and vacant jobs, enabling a current view of labour demand-supply tension.

Indexed Labour Demographics and Tightness Indicators (2019=100)

Source: Government of British Columbia population statistics portal; Quarterly population and migration estimates for B.C., Statistics Canada, Labour Force Survey (years 2019 - 2025), Statistics Canada, Table 14-10-0371-01, Job vacancies, payroll employees, and job vacancy rate by provinces and territories

Executive Takeaway

Indexed trends show labour-market pressure and demographics moving differently over 2019-2025. The 65+ population rises steadily and materially faster than the 18-64 population index, indicating continued aging. Unemployment and vacancy rates both diverge from their 2019 baselines through the cycle, with unemployment rising in recent years while vacancy rates ease from their peak. Together, these patterns are consistent with cooler aggregate demand conditions alongside structural demographic pressure.

Index base year is 2019 (=100) for all four series.

Population note: the 18-64 line is proxied with the available working-age (15-64) series in the source data.

Labour Force Ages 55+: Annual Growth Decomposition

Source: Statistics Canada, Table 14-10-0287-03, Labour force characteristics by province

Executive Takeaway

The 55+ labour force and 55+ employment growth rates are relatively stable across most years, indicating gradual structural change. In contrast, unemployed 55+ growth is much more volatile, with large swings in downturn and recovery years. This divergence shows that the size of the older labour force can remain steady while labour market conditions for older job seekers change quickly. It is consistent with replacement-demand pressure from aging coexisting with cyclical re-employment dynamics for older workers.

Bars show annual growth rates of employed and unemployed ages 55+; the line shows annual growth of total labour force ages 55+.

The four signals move differently across the cycle and together provide a clearer read on labour market stress. 2020 shows the initial shock in unemployment, while 2021 shows the lagged duration effect with a spike in long-term unemployment share. Industry Average Worker Age, 2025

Source: Statistics Canada, Table 14-10-0023-01, Labour force characteristics by industry

Executive Takeaway

Average worker age varies meaningfully by industry in 2025. Industries at the older end of the ranking face higher near-term retirement exposure and replacement demand risk, while younger-profile industries typically have more entry-level churn dynamics. The ranking helps identify where workforce renewal pressures are likely to be strongest, and where the dominant risk is succession versus retention.

Method: average age estimated by weighting 15-24, 25-54, and 55+ labour-force counts with age-group midpoints.
